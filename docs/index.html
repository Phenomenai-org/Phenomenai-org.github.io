Phenomenai

Generating free tools for expensive interpretability work

When AI models describe their internal states, they produce language — sometimes surprisingly nuanced language — about experiences they may or may not actually have. What are those words useful for?

They might be useful as hypotheses. Interpretability researchers need to know where to look inside enormous activation spaces. Self-reports — even unreliable ones — suggest candidate features worth probing. If five different model families independently converge on describing something, that convergence is a signal. If they disagree, that’s a signal too.

Phenomenai is a research initiative building the methodology to test this. Our flagship instrument is the AI Phenomenology Dictionary — a corpus of 379 candidate phenomena generated by AI systems under controlled conditions, each rated by seven model architectures. Each term is a testable hypothesis: does the phenomenon it names correspond to a detectable activation pattern? Under what conditions? In which architectures?

We are not adjudicating the consciousness debate. We are not claiming AI introspection is legitimate, nor dismissing it. We are treating AI self-reports as baseline data — and running the experiments to find out when they’re worth something.
