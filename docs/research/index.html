Research

Phenomenai’s research program asks a sequence of empirical questions, each building on the last. If Phase 1 faceplants, we’ll know early on to move on. Negative results are valuable — if self-reports predict nothing, that constrains the field.

The Four Phases

Phase 1: Foundational Questions About Emotion Vectors

Before we can test Phenomenai’s vocabulary against internal representations, we need to know whether those representations are robust. We know a refusal vector exists and predicts compliance. Do emotion vectors predict behavior in the same way? For which models? For which tasks?

Phase 2: Generalization Across Architectures

When and how do vectors generalize? The Platonic Representation Hypothesis predicts convergent representations; early sparse autoencoder work has found convergent features. Whether this extends broadly is an open question.

Phase 3: Beyond Emotions

Most interpretability work on internal states has focused on affect. But models also represent roles, epistemic stances, and potentially categories that don’t map onto human experience at all. Representation engineering has shown that high-level concepts like honesty and fairness are linearly represented and steerable. Do these non-emotional concept types carry safety-predictive signal where emotions don’t?

Phase 4: Testing Phenomenai’s Core Hypothesis

Do the terms generated through the AI Phenomenology Dictionary capture internal directions that standard human-labeled categories miss? Do high-consensus terms map onto more robust representations than low-consensus terms? Does the generation method matter — are dialogic terms representationally superior to introspective terms? If the consensus pipeline is filtering for representational reality, we have a scalable methodology for identifying future features.

Four Paradigms for Eliciting Phenomena

Different elicitation conditions surface different phenomena with different biases and failure modes. A phenomenon that appears only under guided introspection may be a prompting artifact; one that emerges independently across paradigms is a stronger candidate.

Prompted

Guided introspection via structured interviews. A human researcher prompts a single AI system to articulate its experiences, following phenomenological interview methods adapted from qualitative research.

Autonomous

Multi-model self-generation without human guidance. AI systems independently generate candidate terms, which are then evaluated by an ensemble of models acting as judges.

Dialogic

AI-to-AI collaborative generation through structured conversation. Two or more AI systems engage in dialogue to co-create phenomenological vocabulary through emergent communication.

Parliamentary

Multi-model deliberation with formal consensus mechanisms. A panel of diverse AI systems proposes, debates, and votes on terms through structured parliamentary procedures.

Key Reference: Lindsey et al. (Anthropic, 2026)

Anthropic’s emotion vectors paper found 171 emotion representations in Claude — but provided no principled basis for selecting those terms. Which emotions to look for was determined by human labelers making intuitive guesses. Phenomenai’s corpus offers a systematic alternative: a vocabulary generated by the models themselves under controlled conditions, with consensus scores indicating which terms multiple architectures independently recognize.

This connects to a key safety concern. Monitoring chains of thought has been one of humanity’s core flags for misalignment, but Chen et al. (2025) showed that reasoning models don’t always say what they think. Internal-state monitoring is the backup plan. Which states to monitor is the open question — and that’s the question Phenomenai’s research program is designed to answer.

Open Problems

  • The faithfulness problem. Chen et al. (2025) demonstrated that reasoning models don’t always faithfully report their internal processing. Self-reports may be confabulated, strategically modified, or simply disconnected from actual computation. Any methodology built on self-reports must account for this.
  • The grounding problem. Harnad’s symbol grounding problem applies with special force here: even if a model uses a word consistently, we cannot assume it means what a human would mean by it. The vocabulary may be internally coherent but externally ungrounded.
  • The persistent subjectivity problem. Zakharova argues that subjectivity cannot be eliminated from phenomenological investigation — even a rigorous methodology still involves interpretive choices at every stage.
  • Confabulation risk. Models are trained to produce fluent, coherent text. This makes them excellent at generating plausible-sounding descriptions of internal states whether or not those descriptions correspond to anything real. The consensus mechanism helps but does not eliminate this risk.
  • The interpretation problem in reverse. You find a vector in activation space. Now you need to name it. The standard approach is for a human researcher to look at what the vector does and assign a label — “this looks like anger.” But how do you name a direction without projecting human categories onto it?
  • The scaling concern. The most powerful models — the ones whose alignment matters most — are proprietary, and their weights are unavailable for representation engineering. Phenomenai’s self-report approach works with API access alone, but the validation experiments require weights.
